Asthma Information

What's that?

Hayfever and Asthma

The National Health Survey in 2008 suggests that approximately 1.06 million Victorians suffer from allergic rhinitis (Hayfever) and is 17.6% of the Victorian population.

 

Hay fever can increase your risk of an asthma attack. Research shows that 70-90% of Australians with asthma sufferers also have allergic rhinitis (hayfever) and around 50% of allergic rhinitis suffers also have asthma. This is due to inflamed and sensitized airways are more likely to react to further triggers. Grass pollens can be breathed in and further swell up inflamed airways, leaving you breathless.

What is thunderstrorm asthma?

Thunderstorms Asthma is an infrequent but recurrent asthma epidemic event during high pollen seasons. The epidemic is spread through ruptured pollen particles in the storm clouds at above 96% humidity and transported by strong downdrafts of air back to ground level. Pollen particle sizes of greater than 10µm can be deposited in the eyes and nose while sizes smaller than 5µm could penetrate the lower airway and cause respiratory issues.

 

Who is at risk?

Who are likely to be affected by thunderstorm asthma?

It affects all age groups and severity can range from mild to severe and life-threatening. Environmental, lifestyle, as well as genetic factors, can impact the risk of developing asthma.

  • If you are diagnosed with asthma
  • If you had asthma in the past
  • If you have asthma-like symptoms but not yet diagnosed
  • If you have hay fever
  • If you have hay fever and is allergic to grass pollen will increase your risk to thunderstorm asthma
  • If you have both asthma and hay fever will increase your risk to thunderstorm asthma
  • Even if you don't have a history of asthma or hay fever, it can still affect you
What are the symptoms?

Asthma Symptoms

  • Coughing
  • Difficult to breathe
  • High pitched tone when trying to breathe

Hay Fever Symptoms

  • Itchy nose
  • Itchy eyes
  • Runny nose
  • Sneezing
  • Coughing
Preventative Measures

Basic Prevention

  • Always use your preventer medication as much as the doctor advised. Even if you do not normally use a preventer all year, you should use it every day during September to December if you are going to be in an area where there is ryegrass pollen.
  • Always carry your reliever inhaler (blue puffer) too.
  • Keep up to date with pollen counts and weather forecasts during spring and early summer so you know if a storm is coming.
  • Make sure your written asthma action plan is up to date and includes thunderstorm advice - talk to your doctor.
  • Avoid being outdoors just before and during thunderstorms, especially in wind gusts before the rain. Get inside a building or car with the windows shut and the air conditioner switched to recirculate/ recycled.

Asthma Action Plan

Complete the Asthma Action Plan prepared by the National Asthma Council Australia when visiting your doctor.


Teachers should ensure that parents or guardians complete the Asthma Update Form for their children with asthma to participate in all outdoor school activities.
