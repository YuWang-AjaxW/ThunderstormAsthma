Thunderstorm phenomenas

Lightning and thunder

When the voltage difference between the ground and some storms is about several billions of volts, lightning is generated to overcome the discharge caused by the insulation of the air.

When the air heated by lighting shock expands explosively over 30,000°C, thunder is the sound produced by this phenomenon.

Wind gusts

Thunderstorm leads to rain and hail drag the surrounding air downwards. Raindrops evaporation and melting ice cool the nearby air, to cold dense bubble of air. After landing on the ground, the sinking airflow forms a cool dome, which can quickly spread to the side, producing cool gusts, but may cause damage.

Hail

As the raindrops suspended in the severe rainstorm grow up quickly by "sweeping out" the small cloud droplets that freeze come into contact, hailstorm will form in a thunderstorm.

 

 

Tornadoes

Tornadoes is the rarest but most violent of thunderstorm phenomena. The mass of rapidly rotating air (called a vortex), whose width ranges from a few meters to a kilometer away, extends from the bottom of the storm cloud down in the well-known funnel shape. A weaker tornado, especially in drier conditions, may not be accompanied by a visible funnel.

What conditions could form thunderstorm asthma?

  • Over 200 pollen counts per cubic meter of air (high - extreme)

  • Relative humidity greater than 96%

  • Wind speed of at least 45 - 60km/h

  • Wind gusts between 60 - 90 km/h
